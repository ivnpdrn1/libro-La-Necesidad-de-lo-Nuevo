Chapter 2 — The Useless Wound

Mateo did not go home immediately.
He walked.
Not with urgency. Not with hesitation. Just enough movement to confirm what his body seemed unwilling to deny.
The city continued.
That was the first confirmation.
Nothing had adjusted itself to what had happened. No invisible mechanism had intervened to restore balance. There was no pause, no shift in rhythm, no indication that anything required correction.
Everything held.
Mateo turned a corner and stopped at a crosswalk.
He watched the light change—red to green, green to red—its timing precise, indifferent, consistent.
The system was intact.
He crossed.
Not carefully.
Not recklessly.
Something in between.
A car passed closer than it should have.
Too close.
The driver reacted late.
Mateo felt it—the proximity, the possibility.
He did not step back.
The moment passed.
No escalation.
No consequence.
Mateo continued walking.
His body remembered something.
A trace of what should have followed—a tightening, a withdrawal, a recalibration of distance.
It was there.
But weaker.
Less insistent.
He placed his hand against his side again and pressed.
There was pain.
Clear.
Localized.
But it did not structure him.
That was the difference.
Pain always done more than exist.
It had direction.
It interrupted.
It reorganized behavior.
This did not.
Mateo stopped.
Looked at his hand.
Then it pressed harder.
The sensation increased.
But still not enough.
He let his hand fall.
A group of people passed behind him.
Someone laughed.
Someone argued.
Someone dropped something, picked it up, continued.
The world did not register him.
That, too, felt different.
Not because he expected attention.
Because something in the event demanded weight, and the world was not providing it.
Mateo resumed walking.
At a storefront, he stopped again.
The glass reflected his slightly distortedness, just enough to make the image uncertain.
He studied his reflection.
Looked for evidence.
There should have been something.
A signal.
A visible marker confirming the discontinuity between what had happened and what should have followed.
There was nothing.
He leaned closer.
Examined his face.
His posture.
The way he stood.
Normal.
The word no longer fits.
Mateo straightened.
Stepped back.
The reflection adjusted without resistance.
He turned away.
He was no longer looking for injury.
He was looking for consequence.
And that was harder to find.
He walked past a construction site.
Metal structures.
Loose materials.
A barrier separating movement from risk.
He stopped.
Not because he needed to.
Because something in him wanted to test the boundary.
He stepped closer.
The barrier remained.
He touched it.
It was held.
The structure was still there.
But the meaning of the structure was less clear.
Mateo stepped back.
This was not about breaking things.
Not yet.
It was about noticing.
And what he noticed did not align.
He continued walking until the streets became familiar, until the buildings repeated themselves in ways his memory recognized, until the distance between the event and the present narrowed enough to feel manageable.
He reached his apartment.
Stopped at the door.
It did not open immediately.
He stood there, hand on the handle.
Something had followed him.
Not physically.
Not visible.
A question.
He entered.
Closed the door behind him.
The space was unchanged.
The same objects.
The same arrangement.
The same quiet.
Mateo placed his keys on the table.
Listened.
Nothing responded.
He walked to the kitchen.
Turned on the tap.
Water flowed.
Consistent.
Reliable.
He watched it for a moment.
That still worked.
He turned it off.
Silence returned.
Mateo leaned against the counter.
Closed his eyes.
The fall.
The impact.
The moment after.
Everything aligned.
Except what followed.
He opened his eyes.
Looked at his hands again.
No visible disruption.
No signal.
No demand.
Only presence.
He moved.
Slowly.
Deliberately.
Nothing resisted.
Mateo stopped.
It was not that the wound had disappeared.
It was that it had lost its function.
And without function, it became something else.
Not a warning.
Not a correction.
Just a fact.
He stood there longer than necessary.
Not waiting.
Not deciding.
Trying to understand what it meant for something that should have changed everything…
to change so little.
And in that gap, something began to open.
Not in the world.
In how it was held.
